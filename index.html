Understanding your condition

What is rectal prolapse?

Rectal prolapse occurs when part or all of the rectum — the last section of the large intestine — slips outside the anus. It can be a distressing and uncomfortable condition, but it is more common than many people realise, and there are effective treatments available.

Prolapse is often the result of weakness in the muscles and ligaments that support the pelvic floor. Contributing factors include chronic straining, constipation, aging, and prior childbirth — but it is nobody's fault, and it is nothing to be ashamed of.

Our research has shown that rectal prolapse presents differently between patients — from symptoms that occur only during defecation to prolapse that happens spontaneously throughout the day. Understanding your specific presentation helps guide the right treatment for you.

For many women, symptoms first appear or noticeably worsen during perimenopause or menopause. This is not a coincidence — estrogen plays a direct role in maintaining the strength and elasticity of connective tissue throughout the pelvic floor. As estrogen levels decline, that support can weaken, making existing prolapse more symptomatic or allowing new prolapse to develop. If your symptoms changed around this time in your life, it is worth mentioning to your clinical team — there are hormonal options, including vaginal estrogen, worth exploring.

Common symptoms

Tissue or a bulge protruding from the anus
Feeling of incomplete bowel emptying
Mucus or blood on toilet paper or in stool
Fecal incontinence or urgency
Discomfort, pressure, or pain in the pelvic area
Constipation or difficulty with bowel movements

Rectal prolapse rarely travels alone

Approximately 1 in 3 women with rectal prolapse also have vaginal prolapse or urinary incontinence. These conditions share the same underlying cause — a weakened pelvic floor — and often need to be assessed and treated together. If you have symptoms involving your bladder or vagina alongside bowel symptoms, this is not unusual and it is important to mention. A multidisciplinary team approach, bringing together colorectal surgery, urogynecology, and pelvic floor physiotherapy, typically produces the best outcomes for complex pelvic floor presentations.

Understanding your workup

Diagnostic testing

Not every patient with rectal prolapse needs extensive testing. Many diagnoses are made on clinical history and physical examination alone. When we do order investigations, it is because the result will change what we recommend — not because testing is routine.

The three tests described below are used selectively, based on your specific symptoms, examination findings, and what questions need answering before we can plan the right treatment for you. If a test has been recommended, this page explains what it is, what it looks for, and what to expect.

🧲
MRI Defecography
Dynamic MRI · Defecating proctogram

MRI defecography is an imaging study that captures how your pelvic floor moves during defecation — in real time. Unlike a standard MRI which produces static images, this study asks you to evacuate a gel while the scanner records what happens to the rectum, pelvic floor, and surrounding structures during straining and emptying.

What it shows
The degree of rectal prolapse or intussusception; pelvic floor descent; whether other compartments (bladder, uterus, vagina) prolapse simultaneously; how well the rectum empties; and whether dyssynergic defecation is present — where the pelvic floor fails to relax during a bowel movement.
What to expect
The study is performed in an MRI scanner. A small amount of gel is placed in the rectum beforehand. You will be asked to squeeze, strain, and then evacuate during the scan. It is not painful, but some patients find it uncomfortable or embarrassing. The team performing it are experienced and understand this.
✓ Used selectively
🔊
Dynamic Pelvic Ultrasound
Transperineal or endoanal ultrasound

Ultrasound uses sound waves to produce real-time images of pelvic floor structures. Dynamic ultrasound — performed at the perineum or endoanally — allows us to assess the anal sphincter, rectal wall, and surrounding pelvic floor both at rest and during movement. It is a point-of-care tool that can be performed in the clinic.

What it shows
Sphincter integrity and any defects that may explain incontinence; rectal wall thickness; pelvic floor mobility during straining; and structural abnormalities of the anal canal. It is particularly useful for assessing fecal incontinence and sphincter injury alongside prolapse.
What to expect
Transperineal ultrasound involves a probe placed gently on the skin between the legs — similar to an obstetric ultrasound. Endoanal ultrasound involves a small probe placed just inside the anus. Both are brief, safe, and well tolerated. No bowel preparation is needed.
✓ Used selectively
📊
Anorectal Manometry
ARM · Pressure testing of the anorectum

Anorectal manometry measures the pressures generated by the muscles of the rectum and anal canal. It gives us a detailed picture of how the sphincter muscles are functioning — both at rest and during squeeze — and whether the coordination between the rectum and pelvic floor is normal during defecation.

What it shows
Resting and squeeze anal sphincter pressures; rectal sensation thresholds; the rectoanal inhibitory reflex; and critically, whether dyssynergic defecation is present — a pattern where the sphincter paradoxically contracts rather than relaxing during straining, which is a key target for pelvic floor physiotherapy and biofeedback.
What to expect
A thin, flexible catheter with pressure sensors is placed gently into the rectum. You will be asked to squeeze, push, and cough at various points during the test. It takes approximately 20–30 minutes. It is not painful, though some patients find it slightly uncomfortable. A small enema may be given beforehand.
✓ Used selectively
💡

A note on selective testing. We do not order investigations out of habit. Each test listed here is used when the result will meaningfully change our recommendation — for example, identifying dyssynergic defecation before recommending surgery, or assessing sphincter integrity before planning a repair. If a test has been recommended for you, it is because we believe the answer will help us help you better. If you are unsure why a test has been ordered, please ask — you deserve a clear explanation.

Your options

Treatment options

We believe surgery is the last resort — not the first answer. Here is how we approach treatment, from lifestyle optimisation through to surgical repair.

🌿
First line

Lifestyle & conservative care

For many patients, meaningful improvement comes from dietary and lifestyle changes alone. This is always where we start — and where you have the most power.

  • High-fiber diet (30g/day target)
  • Hydration and bowel habit training
  • Correct toilet posture (squat position)
  • Avoiding straining at stool
  • Weight management where relevant
🧘
Second line

Pelvic floor physiotherapy

A specialist pelvic floor physiotherapist strengthens the muscles supporting your rectum and improves coordination, control, and continence. A multi-society consensus — including ASCRS, ICS, and APTA — now supports this as a core part of treatment.

  • Pelvic floor muscle training
  • Biofeedback therapy
  • Defecation retraining
  • Managing incontinence symptoms
🔬
When indicated

Surgical repair

When prolapse is significant and conservative measures have not provided adequate relief, surgery offers durable repair. Our research shows surgery improves bowel symptoms across a wide range of patients. The right approach depends on your anatomy, symptoms, health, and preferences.

  • Rectopexy — the rectum is lifted and secured; performed laparoscopically (keyhole) or with robotic assistance
  • Ventral mesh rectopexy — rectopexy using synthetic or biologic mesh to reinforce the repair. Synthetic mesh provides strong durable support; biologic graft is an alternative for patients where synthetic mesh is a concern. The choice depends on individual factors and is discussed with your surgeon
  • Posterior suture rectopexy — rectopexy without mesh, for patients who prefer to avoid it
  • Resection rectopexy — rectopexy combined with removal of a portion of the colon, when indicated
  • Perineal procedures — surgery performed through the perineum (Altemeier, Delorme); an option for higher-risk patients
  • Concomitant repair — when vaginal prolapse or urinary incontinence is present alongside rectal prolapse, combined repair with urogynecology or female urology may be appropriate and is discussed as part of multidisciplinary planning
Lifestyle medicine & prolapse

Why your whole life matters — not just your bowel

Lifestyle medicine is a clinical discipline that uses evidence-based behavioural interventions — nutrition, movement, sleep, stress management, substance avoidance, and social connection — as the primary treatment for chronic disease. These same pillars have a direct biological rationale for pelvic floor health and prolapse.

We present each pillar honestly: where the evidence is strong, we say so. Where it is emerging or extrapolated from related conditions, we say that too. Our goal is to give you the full picture — not false certainty.

A note on evidence Direct clinical trials applying lifestyle medicine to rectal prolapse are limited. The strongest evidence exists for pelvic floor physiotherapy and exercise. For other pillars, we draw on biological rationale and evidence from related pelvic floor and bowel conditions — and invite you into this frontier as an informed, active participant in your own care.
🥗
Nutrition & fiber
Your diet shapes your bowel — and your pelvic floor
▼
Strong evidence

Chronic straining is one of the leading contributors to rectal prolapse and pelvic floor damage over time. Straining is almost always a consequence of hard, infrequent stool — which is itself a consequence of insufficient fiber and fluid. A high-fiber diet softens stool, reduces transit time, and directly lowers the mechanical load on pelvic floor structures with every bowel movement. Fermented foods support gut microbiome diversity, which influences motility and stool consistency. Anti-inflammatory eating may reduce systemic inflammation that affects connective tissue integrity — the very tissue that holds the rectum in place.

Target 25–30g of fiber daily from whole food sources — vegetables, legumes, fruit, whole grains
Drink 1.5–2L of water daily — fiber without fluid can worsen constipation
Include fermented foods regularly — yoghurt, kefir, kimchi, sauerkraut, miso
Reduce ultra-processed foods, which are low in fiber and high in additives that disrupt gut motility
Never ignore the urge to defecate — delayed emptying increases straining
🚶
Physical activity & movement
Movement drives motility. Targeted exercise rebuilds support
▼
Strong evidence for PFPT & exercise

Physical activity stimulates bowel motility through direct mechanical effects and through autonomic nervous system regulation — regular movement is one of the most reliable treatments for constipation. Pelvic floor physical therapy (PFPT) has the strongest evidence base of any lifestyle intervention for prolapse and bowel dysfunction, supported by multi-society consensus. Beyond PFPT, core stability and low-impact aerobic exercise support the muscular and connective tissue environment of the pelvic floor. High-impact activity and heavy lifting that increases intra-abdominal pressure should be approached carefully and ideally guided by a physiotherapist.

Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week — the standard lifestyle medicine recommendation. Walking, swimming, and cycling are all excellent starting points
Include resistance training at least twice a week — muscle strength supports the structural environment of the pelvic floor and is important for long-term health, especially after menopause
Pelvic floor physiotherapy — the single most evidence-based lifestyle intervention for prolapse and bowel dysfunction
Core strengthening exercises, guided by your physiotherapist, to support the pelvic floor
Correct toilet posture — a squat position (knees above hips, using a footstool) reduces straining significantly
Avoid heavy lifting and high-impact exercise without physiotherapy clearance — guidance on safe activities is an active area of our research
🌙
Restorative sleep
Sleep repairs tissue. Poor sleep inflames it
▼
Biological rationale — emerging evidence

Sleep is when the body repairs connective tissue — including collagen, the structural protein that gives the pelvic floor its strength and elasticity. Chronic sleep deprivation elevates cortisol, promotes systemic inflammation, and impairs tissue repair mechanisms. Poor sleep is also independently associated with worsened bowel symptoms: disrupted circadian rhythms alter gut motility and can worsen constipation and urgency. While direct trials on sleep and prolapse are lacking, the mechanistic links are strong enough that restorative sleep should be considered a genuine part of pelvic floor care.

Aim for 7–9 hours of quality sleep per night consistently
Maintain a regular sleep-wake schedule, including weekends
Limit screens and bright light in the hour before bed to support melatonin production
If you have sleep apnea or chronic insomnia, seek treatment — untreated sleep disorders have systemic consequences
Notice whether your bowel symptoms are worse after poor sleep nights — many patients find a direct connection
🧠
Stress management
The gut-brain axis is real — and bidirectional
▼
Emerging evidence

Chronic psychological stress activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, elevating cortisol and promoting inflammation — both of which impair connective tissue integrity over time. The gut-brain axis is well-established: stress directly alters gut motility, can trigger constipation or urgency, and worsens bowel symptoms in patients with pelvic floor disorders. Dyssynergic defecation — where the pelvic floor muscles fail to relax properly during a bowel movement — has known associations with anxiety and autonomic dysregulation. Managing stress is not an optional extra; it is part of the physiological environment your pelvic floor lives in.

Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) has the strongest evidence for gut symptom improvement
Diaphragmatic breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system and can directly reduce pelvic floor tension
Yoga and gentle movement practices that combine breath and body awareness are particularly relevant
If anxiety or low mood is significantly impacting your quality of life, speak with your GP — psychological support is a clinical intervention, not a luxury
Notice the connection between stressful periods and changes in your bowel habits — awareness is the first step
🚭
Avoidance of risky substances
Smoking and alcohol have direct pelvic floor consequences
▼
Strong evidence for smoking

Smoking is directly harmful to pelvic floor health through two mechanisms: chronic cough dramatically increases intra-abdominal pressure with every episode, mechanically stressing the pelvic floor structures repeatedly over years; and smoking impairs collagen synthesis and accelerates connective tissue degradation, weakening the very scaffolding that holds pelvic organs in place. The evidence here is robust. Alcohol is relevant primarily through its effects on bowel function — alcohol is a gut irritant that disrupts motility, can worsen urgency and diarrhoea, and in chronic heavy use promotes systemic inflammation and poor nutritional status.

Stopping smoking is one of the single most impactful things you can do for your pelvic floor — and for your surgical outcomes
Ask your GP about smoking cessation support — combination pharmacotherapy and behavioural support is significantly more effective than willpower alone
Treat a chronic cough as a pelvic floor issue — persistent cough from any cause increases prolapse risk and should be investigated and managed
If you drink alcohol, notice its effects on your bowel symptoms — reducing intake often leads to noticeable improvement in urgency and frequency
Before surgery, stopping smoking is mandatory — it significantly increases wound and anastomotic complication risk
🤝
Social connection & mental health
Isolation amplifies symptoms. Connection supports recovery
▼
Biological rationale — underexplored in prolapse

Rectal prolapse carries a significant and often invisible psychological burden. Symptoms including incontinence, urgency, and prolapse itself cause shame, social withdrawal, and avoidance of activities that bring joy — all of which worsen quality of life and, through the stress and inflammation pathways described above, can worsen physical symptoms in return. Social isolation is an independent risk factor for poor health outcomes across almost every chronic condition studied. Our own research has shown that patients with psychiatric comorbidities benefit from prolapse repair — but they often arrive to care later, having suffered longer in silence. You deserve to be in the room, asking the question, and receiving care.

Talk to someone you trust about what you are experiencing — isolation is not a symptom you have to manage alone
Peer support groups for pelvic floor disorders exist and are genuinely helpful for many patients
If you are avoiding social activities because of symptoms, tell your clinical team — there are often practical solutions
Screen yourself honestly for anxiety and depression — these conditions are highly prevalent in patients with pelvic floor disorders and are treatable
Recovery is not only physical. Investing in your relationships and mental health is part of getting better
🌸
Hormonal health
Estrogen shapes the pelvic floor. Its decline matters
▼
Emerging evidence — discuss with your clinician

Estrogen is not just a reproductive hormone — it is a key regulator of connective tissue integrity throughout the body, including the muscles, ligaments, and fascial structures of the pelvic floor. During perimenopause and menopause, declining estrogen leads to changes in collagen composition and tissue elasticity that can weaken pelvic floor support, making existing prolapse more symptomatic or allowing new prolapse to develop. Many women notice their bowel symptoms and prolapse worsen significantly around this time — this is a recognised physiological pattern, not coincidence. Vaginal atrophy, the thinning and drying of vaginal and rectal mucosa due to estrogen loss, can also worsen bowel symptoms including urgency, incomplete emptying, and discomfort. Local vaginal estrogen is a low-risk, locally acting treatment that can improve tissue quality and alleviate some of these symptoms without the systemic exposure of oral hormone therapy — and it is widely underused in women with pelvic floor disorders. Systemic hormone replacement therapy (HRT) is a broader individual risk-benefit conversation with a clinician. Direct trial evidence specifically linking HRT to rectal prolapse outcomes is limited, but the mechanistic rationale for hormonal optimisation as part of pelvic floor care is strong.

If your symptoms worsened around perimenopause or menopause, raise this explicitly with your clinical team — it is clinically relevant
Ask about vaginal estrogen specifically — it is local, low-risk, and often underused in women with pelvic floor and bowel symptoms
Discuss systemic HRT with your GP or menopause specialist if you have broader perimenopausal symptoms — the decision is individual and nuanced
PFPT and hormonal optimisation work well together — one strengthens the muscles, the other supports the tissue they live in
Do not assume hormonal options are off the table without a conversation — many women have never been offered them in the context of pelvic floor symptoms

How to start integrating lifestyle medicine

You do not need to change everything at once. Research consistently shows that starting with one or two sustainable changes — and building from there — produces better long-term outcomes than attempting a wholesale transformation.

01
Start with fiber and movement
These have the strongest direct evidence and the most immediate impact on bowel symptoms. Increase fiber gradually to avoid bloating, and begin walking daily. These two changes alone can transform bowel function.
02
See a pelvic floor physiotherapist
This is the highest-evidence lifestyle intervention available for prolapse and bowel dysfunction. Ask your GP or surgical team for a referral if you have not already been seen. Do not wait until surgery is decided.
03
Address one more pillar every 4–6 weeks
Once fiber and movement feel habitual, turn your attention to sleep, then stress, then substances. Each pillar reinforces the others — better sleep improves motivation for movement; less stress improves sleep. Build the whole picture gradually.

About this score

The Wexner (Cleveland Clinic) Continence Score is one of the most widely used and validated instruments for measuring fecal incontinence severity. Scores range from 0 (perfect continence) to 20 (complete incontinence). A score of 0 indicates no incontinence; scores above 9 are generally considered clinically significant. It is used in our published research and in prolapse registries worldwide.

About this score

The Obstructed Defecation Score (ODS Score, Renzi et al.) measures the severity of obstructed defecation — difficulty emptying the bowel despite the urge to do so. Scores range from 0 to 31. Scores of 0–8 are mild, 9–13 moderate, and 14 or above severe. It is widely used in the assessment and follow-up of patients with rectal prolapse, rectocele, and pelvic floor dysfunction.

Factors associated with recurrence

We cannot give you a single number for your personal recurrence risk — no validated prediction model yet exists for rectal prolapse (this is one of the top priorities identified in our Delphi research). What the literature does show is which factors are associated with higher or lower risk, and which are within your power to modify.

Associated with higher risk
Factors the evidence flags
Younger age — not because surgery works less well, but because a longer lifetime ahead means more years during which recurrence can occur
Older age (>80) — higher overall surgical risk and frailty may influence approach and recovery
Connective tissue disorders and hypermobility syndromes (e.g. Ehlers-Danlos) — underlying tissue fragility affects repair durability
Perineal approach — recurrence rates are higher than abdominal rectopexy, though perineal procedures are often chosen deliberately for higher-risk patients
Prior prolapse repair — recurrent prolapse carries higher recurrence risk than primary repair
Associated with lower risk
Protective factors
Abdominal rectopexy (laparoscopic or robotic)

Within your power to change
Modifiable factors — where lifestyle medicine matters most
Stopping smoking before surgery

Before your operation

Preparing for surgery

Good preparation makes a real difference to how you recover. Here is a timeline of what to expect, and a checklist to track your readiness.

1
6–8 weeks before

Lifestyle optimisation

This is your most important window. Changes made now directly influence your recovery — and habits built here tend to stick long after surgery.
Increase dietary fiber to 25–30g/day and ensure adequate hydration
Begin or continue pelvic floor physiotherapy
Start a daily walking habit — 30 minutes is the target. This is an ideal time to make movement a routine, not a plan
Learn diaphragmatic breathing and begin a simple stress reduction practice — breath work activates the parasympathetic nervous system and directly reduces pelvic floor tension. Even 5–10 minutes daily makes a difference
If you smoke, stop now — smoking significantly increases complication risk
2
2–4 weeks before

Pre-operative assessment

You will attend a pre-admission clinic where blood tests, an ECG, and an anaesthetic assessment are completed.
Bring a full list of your current medications

Life after surgery

Recovery from rectopexy is gradual. Most patients are surprised by how quickly they improve — and by how much the right habits matter in the weeks and months that follow.

Week 1–2

Immediate recovery

You will typically spend 1–2 nights in hospital. Pain is manageable with regular oral analgesia, and most patients are mobile within hours of surgery.

Your experience matters to research

We are still in the early stages of understanding how lifestyle, exercise, and psychological wellbeing affect rectal prolapse. The honest truth is that we need patients to help build this evidence — through surveys, interviews, registry participation, and long-term follow-up.

Our international Delphi consensus involving 372 clinicians found that long-term functional outcomes are the single highest research priority for prolapse care. That evidence only becomes possible when patients agree to be followed over time.
